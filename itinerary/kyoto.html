N°01 Japan Five days

Kyoto

35.0116° N — 135.7681° E

Five days on the old capital’s quiet side — dawn shrines before the crowds, raked gravel at opening bell, tea poured slowly, and the long amber evenings of Higashiyama.

The itinerary

I

The brief

Kyoto rewards the early riser and quietly punishes the itinerary that tries too hard. Two thousand temples will not be seen; a dozen, taken slowly, will not be forgotten.

This route is built around mornings. The great sights — the vermilion gates, the gravel gardens — belong to whoever arrives at first light, and Kyoto’s first light belongs to almost no one. Afternoons are left deliberately loose: tea, side streets, a river bank. The city does the rest.

Base yourself east, in Higashiyama, where the lanes still run on lantern-light after dusk. Everything in these five days is reachable by train, bus or on foot — the single day trip, to the tea town of Uji, is twenty minutes down the Nara line.

II

Day by day

Day 01 of 5

Arrival — the lantern hour

Land, train in, and resist the urge to begin. The first evening is for one street done properly: Ishibei-kōji at dusk, when the stone lane lights up and the tour groups have gone down the hill.

  • 15:00 Keys to the machiya — shoes off, paper light, tatami quiet.
  • 17:30 Ishibei-kōji and the Yasaka pagoda as the lanterns come on.
  • 19:00 Counter kaiseki, first of the trip. Trust the chef entirely.

Day 02 of 5

Ten thousand gates

Fushimi Inari is a different mountain at dawn — above the Yotsutsuji viewpoint the crowds thin to nothing and the gates run on through the cedar in silence. Come down for coffee, then ride the Nara line to Uji, where tea is still ground by stone.

  • 05:40 Into the torii tunnels before the city wakes.
  • 07:30 Yotsutsuji overlook — the whole basin under morning haze.
  • 11:00 Uji: matcha at a shop older than the country you flew in from.
  • 15:30 Byōdō-in’s phoenix hall, gold on a grey pond.
  • 19:00 Back in Gion for the evening — nowhere to be.

Day 03 of 5

West, into the bamboo

Cross the city early to Arashiyama, where the grove still creaks in the wind before ten. The finer garden is above it — Ōkōchi Sansō, an actor’s life’s work — then the Ōi river, and the mossed quiet of Gio-ji in the late afternoon.

  • 07:00 The bamboo grove while it still belongs to the wind.
  • 09:30 Ōkōchi Sansō — matcha included, crowds not.
  • 13:00 The river: flat boats, herons, the bridge from a teahouse seat.
  • 16:00 Gio-ji’s moss garden, an acre of green shade.
  • 19:30 Yudōfu — tofu, done seriously — near Tenryū-ji.

Day 04 of 5

The garden argument

Ryōan-ji’s fifteen stones are arranged so that one is always hidden — count them and see. Spend the morning with the Zen gardens of the northwest, then let the afternoon fall apart agreeably in the Nishiki market and a proper hour of tea.

  • 08:00 Ryōan-ji at opening bell. Sit. Count. Fail.
  • 10:30 Daitoku-ji’s subtemples — gravel raked into weather.
  • 14:00 Nishiki market: pickles, knives, tiny grilled things.
  • 17:00 A tea ceremony taken slowly, a full hour of it.
  • 21:00 Nightcap in Pontochō alley, one lantern wide.

Day 05 of 5

Last light

One quiet walk to close: the Philosopher’s Path north under the last of the maples to Ginkaku-ji, the silver pavilion that was never silvered — and is better for it.

  • 07:30 Philosopher’s Path, canal-side, almost alone.
  • 09:00 Ginkaku-ji and its moon-viewing sand cone.
  • 11:00 A last bowl of soba near the station.
  • 13:30 Haruka Express back to KIX.

III — Field note

You do not see Kyoto by covering ground. You see it by standing still in the right courtyard at the right hour.
IV

Essentials

Getting there

Fly into Kansai (KIX). The Haruka Express runs direct to Kyoto Station in 75 minutes; an IC card handles every train and bus after that. From Tokyo, the shinkansen does it in two and a quarter hours.

Staying

A machiya — a restored wooden townhouse — in Higashiyama or Gion puts the lantern lanes outside your door. Book dinner where you sleep the first night; the good counters fill weeks out.

Notes

Temples close early, most by 17:00 — spend mornings on them and keep afternoons loose. Carry coins for shrine offerings and old-school ticket machines. Late November evenings are cold; the maples are worth it.
